Day 1: Belief vs. Knowledge

Question: What do I believe, and how do I know it’s true?
Exercise: Pick one strong belief you hold. Ask: Do I have evidence, or am I just convinced? Write down the difference.


---

Day 2: Sources of Knowledge

Question: Where does my knowledge come from—experience, books, teachers, intuition?
Exercise: Make a list of your top 5 “trusted sources.” For each, evaluate why you trust them.


---

Day 3: Perception Check

Question: How accurate are my senses?
Exercise: Observe a common object or event. Write down everything you perceive. Then check for details you missed or misinterpreted.


---

Day 4: Bias Awareness

Question: What biases shape what I think I know?
Exercise: List one belief you hold strongly. Ask: Would I believe this if I grew up in a completely different environment?


---

Day 5: Distinguishing Opinion from Knowledge

Question: Which of my beliefs are opinions, and which are knowledge?
Exercise: Take 3 beliefs. Categorize them: Opinion / Knowledge / Uncertain. Reflect why some are uncertain.


---

Day 6: Evidence Evaluation

Question: What counts as sufficient evidence for me to believe something?
Exercise: Pick a claim online. Analyze: Is the evidence strong, weak, anecdotal, or logical?


---

Day 7: Limits of Knowledge

Question: Are there things I can never know?
Exercise: Reflect on a question you’ve tried to answer for a long time but can’t. Accept uncertainty—note your mental resistance.


---

Day 8: Memory and Reliability

Question: How reliable is my memory?
Exercise: Recall a detailed past event. Compare your recollection to any records or other people’s accounts. Note differences.


---

Day 9: Learning from Others

Question: Can I trust testimony or authority?
Exercise: Take one expert claim. Research alternative perspectives. Decide if you’ll revise your belief.


---

Day 10: Thinking vs. Knowing

Question: Am I thinking clearly, or just repeating ideas I’ve heard?
Exercise: Take a recent thought or argument. Break it down: premises → logic → conclusion. Spot gaps.


---

Day 11: Conceptual Clarity

Question: Do I understand the words and ideas I use?
Exercise: Pick 2 abstract words (e.g., “freedom” or “justice”). Write down your precise definitions. Compare with others’ definitions.


---

Day 12: Probability and Uncertainty

Question: How confident am I in what I “know”?
Exercise: For 3 beliefs, assign confidence from 0–100%. Ask: Could I be wrong?


---

Day 13: Skepticism

Question: How can I avoid being deceived by false knowledge?
Exercise: Find one “fact” online. Research for contradictions. Note how skepticism changes your belief.


---

Day 14: Knowledge in Action

Question: How does what I know guide what I do?
Exercise: Take one decision today. Ask: What knowledge informs this choice? How reliable is it? Act consciously.

Tips for Maximum Effect

Journal your reflections daily.

Don’t rush for answers—focus on observing and analyzing.

Combine with reading short epistemology texts (Descartes, Locke, or modern thinkers like Sam Harris on reasoning).
